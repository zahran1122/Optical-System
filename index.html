- Plane Mirror -

Image 5 shows a child standing in front of a plane mirror. We can clearly see the shape of the child's face and body through the mirror reflection. The image that appears in the mirror is caused by light reflecting off the mirror surface and reaching our eyes (See image 6).

- Image 5. A student looking in a mirror -

- Image 6. Formation of an image on a plane mirror -

The light in the room reflects from your body to the mirror. On the mirror, you will see your reflection because the reflected light from the mirror reaches your eyes. When light rays strike a smooth and flat surface, the reflected rays are parallel to each other. Image 7 shows a ray leaving the top of the object. This ray is then reflected from the mirror (the angle of reflection equals the angle of incidence) and received by the eye. From the eye's perspective, it appears as if the ray is coming from behind the mirror. Image 8 shows two light rays leaving the top of the object. Actually, rays spread in all directions from every point on the object, but only a small portion of the rays reach the eye.

- Image 7. Path of light rays -

- Image 8. Rays seem to originate from behind the mirror -

All rays leaving a specific point on the object, regardless of the angle \(\theta\) to the mirror, appear to originate from the corresponding point on the reflection behind the mirror. For every point on the object, there is a corresponding point on the reflection, and this is why the reflection in a plane mirror appears clear and undistorted. In this context, "undistorted" means the image seen in the mirror does not undergo any change in shape or size. In other words, the image in a plane mirror appears the same as the original object without any distortion or change in shape. Thus, the image remains proportional and accurate, just like the actual object.

- Spherical Mirrors -

Spherical mirrors are mirrors that have a reflective surface in the form of a curve. Generally, spherical mirrors have a surface similar to a sphere (\(spherical\)). The surface of a spherical mirror can be concave or convex. Observe Image 9! If the inner surface of the spherical mirror is polished, it is called a concave mirror. If the outer surface is polished, it is called a convex mirror. Although they originate from the same mirror, there are significant differences between concave and convex mirrors.

- Image 9. Spherical, concave, and convex mirrors -

Each type of spherical mirror, like a plane mirror, follows the law of light reflection. The difference lies in elements such as the center of curvature (M), the focal point or focus (F), the vertex or intersection of the principal axis with the mirror (O), the radius of curvature or distance from point O to point M (R), and the focal length or distance from point O to point F (f).

- Concave Mirror -

A concave mirror is a type of spherical mirror with a reflective surface on the inside. The concave mirror has three names: concave, converging, and positive. It is called concave or \(concave\) because of its cave-like shape (\(cave\)). It is called converging and positive because it converges light. As shown in Image 10, light rays incident on a concave mirror will be reflected toward the focal point, gathering the light at one point.

- Image 10. Ray diagram of a concave mirror -

One common application of concave mirrors in daily life is the makeup or shaving mirror, due to its ability to magnify images, allowing details to be more visible. Additionally, concave mirrors are used in reflector telescopes to collect light from distant objects (such as stars) and focus the light at the focal point to produce a clearer image.

A convex mirror is a type of spherical mirror where the reflective surface is on the outside. A convex mirror has three names: convex mirror, diverging mirror, and negative mirror. It is called a convex mirror because its surface bulges outward, similar to the outer part of a sphere. It is also called a diverging and negative mirror because it disperses light. As shown in Figure 15, light rays incident on a convex mirror are reflected outward, away from the focal point.

- Figure 15. Ray Diagram for a Convex Mirror -

Although the light spreads away from the focal point, it doesn't mean that an image won't form. The meaning of spreading away from the focal point in a convex mirror is that the light rays incident and reflected by the convex mirror will spread away from each other. In a convex mirror, these reflected rays appear to originate from a focal point behind the mirror, but they never actually meet at one point. As a result, the image formed by a convex mirror is always virtual, upright, and smaller than the actual object. Even though the light rays spread, our eyes perceive them as if they are coming from the focal point behind the mirror, so an image still forms even though it is not real and is smaller than the actual object.

Part 2: Refraction of Light

Snell's Law of Refraction

Light travels through a vacuum at a speed of \(c\) = \(3\cdot10^8\) \(m\)\(/\)\(s\). Light can also travel through many materials, such as air, water, and glass. The atoms in these materials can absorb, re-emit, and scatter light. Therefore, light travels through materials at a speed slower than \(c\), with the actual speed depending on the material's properties. In general, we will see that the change in speed as light moves from one material to another causes the light ray to deviate from its original direction. This change in direction is called refraction.

- Figure 17. Refraction from Air to Water -

- Figure 18. Refraction from Water to Air -

To illustrate the extent to which the speed of light in a material differs from its speed in a vacuum, we use a parameter called the refractive index. The refractive index is an important parameter because it appears in Snell's Law of refraction, which will be discussed in the next section. This law is the foundation of all the phenomena discussed in this chapter. The refractive index of a material \( n \) is the ratio of the speed of light in a vacuum \( c \) to the speed of light in the material \( v \):

$$n = \frac{\text{Speed of light in vacuum}}{\text{Speed of light in material}} = \frac{c}{v}$$

When light strikes the surface between two transparent media, such as air and water, the light generally splits into two, as seen in Figure 17. Some of the light is reflected with the angle of reflection equal to the angle of incidence. The rest will be transmitted through the surface. If the incident light is not parallel to the normal line, the transmitted or refracted light will have a different direction from the incident light. The light passing through the second medium is said to be refracted and behaves

  1. When light moves from a medium with a lower refractive index, the refracted ray will bend towards the normal line, as shown in Figure 17.
  2. When light moves from a medium with a higher refractive index, the refracted ray will bend away from the normal line, as shown in Figure 18.

These two possibilities illustrate that the situation in Figure 17 can be reversed to produce Figure 18. Note that \(n_1\) and \(n_2\) in each case are different. The angle of refraction \(\theta_2\) depends on the angle of incidence \(\theta_1\) and the refractive indices, \(n_2\) and \(n_1\), of the two media. The relationship between these quantities is known as Snell's Law. Here is the mathematical equation:

$$n_1 \sin \theta_1 = n_2 \sin \theta_2$$

- Figure 19. Total Internal Reflection -

In cases where light passes through two media, there is a phenomenon known as "total internal reflection." This occurs when all the light arriving at a certain angle within a medium with a higher refractive index is reflected back into that medium, with no light passing into the medium with a lower refractive index. This phenomenon can happen when the angle of incidence is equal to the critical angle \(\theta_c\). As a result, the angle of refraction will be \(90^\circ\) relative to the normal line.

-- Lenses --

Lenses are used in various optical devices, such as eyeglasses, cameras, and telescopes, to help form images of objects we see. Lenses are made of transparent materials that can bend light as it passes through, a process known as refraction. Suppose there is an object very far from the lens. Light from this object comes in the form of parallel rays towards the lens. When these rays pass through the lens, they are bent so that they converge at a single point. If the lens is of good quality, all rays will converge at the same point, resulting in a sharp image. However, if the lens is not well-designed, these rays may not converge at a single point, and the resulting image can be blurred.

- Figure 16. Diagram of rays captured by the eye -

There are two main types of lenses commonly used:

  1. \(Converging\) \(Lens\): This lens has a shape that causes parallel rays entering it to converge at a single point after passing through the lens. The point where these rays converge is called the focal point (F), and the distance from the lens to this focal point is called the focal length (f). Converging lenses are often used to make images larger and clearer.
